Glossary

Plain-language definitions for the numbers and labels you see around AlForks — what they mean, and how the app actually works them out.

Time & distance Elevation Records & comparisons Training & fitness

Time & distance

Riding timemoving time
The time you were actually moving under your own power. Pauses (stopped at a junction, fixing a flat, lunch) and any lift/shuttle segments are taken out.
Elapsed time
Total wall-clock time from the first GPS point to the last — everything included: stops, breaks, and lift rides. Usually larger than riding time.
Average speed
Distance divided by riding time, not elapsed time. Lift and shuttle segments are excluded from both the distance and the time, so the number reflects your pace when you were actually riding — not a number watered down by chairlift rides and coffee stops.

Elevation

Climbingelevation gain
Total metres climbed over the activity, added up across every uphill section. Only climbing you did under your own power counts — see Assisted segments.
Descentvertical · elevation loss
Total metres descended. On skis and snowboard this is the headline number (shown as Vertical), since a ski day is mostly about the down. Descent always counts — even on a lift-served run, you still rode it down.
Assisted segmentslift / shuttle
Stretches where you were carried uphill rather than moving under your own power — a chairlift, gondola, T-bar, or a shuttle/car bump back to the top. The app spots these automatically.
Assisted stretches are left out of riding distance, riding time, average speed and climbing, so those stats stay honest. Descent still counts (the down is yours), and the segment is marked with a LIFT tag on the activity.

Records & comparisons

Top performanceslast 365 days
Your best result in each category — longest, most climbing, fastest, and so on — from the last 365 days only. A rolling "how have I been doing lately" snapshot that refreshes as the year rolls forward.
All-time records
Your best ever in each category, across every activity you've logged. These don't expire — beating one is a genuine personal best.
Year-over-yearYoY
A comparison of one calendar year against the previous one for a given metric (e.g. distance this year vs last year), so you can see your trend across seasons.
“vs prior 365d”
The small up/down figure under the dashboard's headline numbers. It compares the last 365 days with the 365 days before that — a like-for-like rolling window, not a partial calendar year.

Training & fitness

Heart-rate zonesKarvonen
Five effort bands derived from your heart rate. AlForks uses the Karvonen method, which sets the bands from your heart-rate reserve — the gap between your resting and maximum HR — so the zones fit you rather than a generic chart. Higher zone = harder effort.
Set your resting and max HR in Settings → Fitness for accurate zones. Without HR data the app falls back to an estimate from duration, distance and climbing.
Training load
A single "how hard was this?" score per activity. With heart rate, it's zone-weighted — easy time barely counts, hard time counts a lot (recovery below ~60% effort adds nothing). It's the daily fuel for Fitness, Fatigue and Form.
FitnessCTL
Your training load smoothed over roughly the last 6 weeks (a 42-day weighted average). It rises slowly as you put in consistent work and fades when you ease off — a measure of the engine you've built.
FatigueATL
The same training load but smoothed over just the last week (a 7-day weighted average). It spikes fast after hard days and settles quickly with rest — how tired you are right now.
FormTSB
Fitness minus Fatigue — your freshness. Positive means rested and ready (you've recovered faster than you've built fitness); negative means you're carrying fatigue from recent hard work. Useful for timing a big day.
Load ratioACWR
Acute-to-chronic workload ratio: your last 7 days of load against your last 28 days. A rough gauge of how fast you're ramping up.
Above 1.5 = ramping hard (higher injury risk); roughly 0.8–1.5 = a sustainable sweet spot; below 0.8 = detraining. Needs about a month of history before it's meaningful.
VO₂max
An estimate of your aerobic capacity — how much oxygen your body can use when working hard. AlForks uses a simple field formula from the ratio of your maximum to resting heart rate, so it reflects your whole-body fitness (cycling, skiing, hiking) rather than a running-only watch estimate.
It's a ballpark trend line, not a lab measurement — watch the direction over time, not the exact number.
