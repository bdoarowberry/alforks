Glossary

Plain-language definitions for the numbers and labels you see around AlForks — what they mean, and how the app actually works them out.

Time & distance Elevation Records & comparisons Heart rate Training & fitness Body & wellness Trails & routes Data quality

Time & distance

Riding timemoving time
The time you were actually moving under your own power. Pauses (stopped at a junction, fixing a flat, lunch) and any lift/shuttle segments are taken out.
Elapsed time
Total wall-clock time from the first GPS point to the last — everything included: stops, breaks, and lift rides. Usually larger than riding time.
Average speed
Distance divided by riding time, not elapsed time. Lift and shuttle segments are excluded from both the distance and the time, so the number reflects your pace when you were actually riding — not a number watered down by chairlift rides and coffee stops.
Distanceriding distance
How far you travelled under your own power. Like average speed and climbing, it leaves out assisted stretches — so a chairlift ride or a shuttle back to the trailhead doesn't pad your distance.
Top speedmax speed
The fastest you went. Records and headline numbers use your fastest riding moment — lift and shuttle samples are excluded, so a gondola or a drive uphill never shows up as a "top speed".
A few places (e.g. an individual activity's raw readout) show the whole-track maximum instead, which can read higher if a lift moved faster than you rode.

Elevation

Climbingelevation gain
Total metres climbed over the activity, added up across every uphill section. Only climbing you did under your own power counts — see Assisted segments.
Descentvertical · elevation loss
Total metres descended. On skis and snowboard this is the headline number (shown as Vertical), since a ski day is mostly about the down. Descent always counts — even on a lift-served run, you still rode it down.
Assisted segmentslift / shuttle
Stretches where you were carried uphill rather than moving under your own power — a chairlift, gondola, T-bar, or a shuttle/car bump back to the top. The app spots these automatically.
Assisted stretches are left out of riding distance, riding time, average speed and climbing, so those stats stay honest. Descent still counts (the down is yours), and the segment is marked with a LIFT tag on the activity.
Peak elevationhigh point
The highest point reached on the activity, taken straight from the GPS track's elevation data.
Gradientgrade · steepness
How steep the terrain is at a point on the track — the change in elevation over the distance covered, shown as a percentage on the elevation and speed profiles. Positive is uphill, negative downhill.

Records & comparisons

Top performanceslast 365 days
Your best result in each category — longest, most climbing, fastest, and so on — from the last 365 days only. A rolling "how have I been doing lately" snapshot that refreshes as the year rolls forward.
All-time records
Your best ever in each category, across every activity you've logged. These don't expire — beating one is a genuine personal best.
Year-over-yearYoY
A comparison of one calendar year against the previous one for a given metric (e.g. distance this year vs last year), so you can see your trend across seasons.
“vs prior 365d”
The small up/down figure under the dashboard's headline numbers. It compares the last 365 days with the 365 days before that — a like-for-like rolling window, not a partial calendar year.
Record categoriesLongest · Biggest Day · …
The specific bests AlForks tracks: Longest (distance), Climbing and Vertical (descent), Top speed, longest Duration, and snow-day extras like Biggest Day, Most Lifts in a day and Days on Snow. Each shows as both a last-365-day and an all-time figure.
Streakscurrent · longest
A run of consecutive days with at least one activity. Current streak counts back from today (or yesterday); longest streak is your best ever, across all history.
Active days
The number of distinct calendar days you logged something — not the number of activities. Two rides on the same day count as one active day.

Heart rate

Average HRavg HR · bpm
Your mean heart rate over the activity, in beats per minute. Needs heart-rate data — from a paired Garmin (Settings → Connect Garmin) or a recording that already carries HR.
Max HRpeak HR · bpm
The highest heart rate recorded during the activity. Different from your maximum HR setting, which is the ceiling used to size your zones — you can override that in Settings → Fitness.
Resting HRRHR · “usual”
Your heart rate at rest, from Garmin wellness data. The “usual” figure beside it is your median resting HR over the last 30 days — a baseline to compare today against. A resting HR drifting below your usual is generally a good sign.
Heart-rate zonesKarvonen
Five effort bands derived from your heart rate. AlForks uses the Karvonen method, which sets the bands from your heart-rate reserve — the gap between your resting and maximum HR — so the zones fit you rather than a generic chart. Higher zone = harder effort.
Set your resting and max HR in Settings → Fitness for accurate zones. Without HR data the app falls back to an estimate from duration, distance and climbing.

Training & fitness

Training load
A single "how hard was this?" score per activity. With heart rate, it's zone-weighted — easy time barely counts, hard time counts a lot (recovery below ~60% effort adds nothing). It's the daily fuel for Fitness, Fatigue and Form.
FitnessCTL
Your training load smoothed over roughly the last 6 weeks (a 42-day weighted average). It rises slowly as you put in consistent work and fades when you ease off — a measure of the engine you've built.
FatigueATL
The same training load but smoothed over just the last week (a 7-day weighted average). It spikes fast after hard days and settles quickly with rest — how tired you are right now.
FormTSB
Fitness minus Fatigue — your freshness. Positive means rested and ready (you've recovered faster than you've built fitness); negative means you're carrying fatigue from recent hard work. Useful for timing a big day.
Load ratioACWR
Acute-to-chronic workload ratio: your last 7 days of load against your last 28 days. A rough gauge of how fast you're ramping up.
Above 1.5 = ramping hard (higher injury risk); roughly 0.8–1.5 = a sustainable sweet spot; below 0.8 = detraining. Needs about a month of history before it's meaningful.
VO₂max
An estimate of your aerobic capacity — how much oxygen your body can use when working hard. AlForks uses a simple field formula from the ratio of your maximum to resting heart rate, so it reflects your whole-body fitness (cycling, skiing, hiking) rather than a running-only watch estimate.
It's a ballpark trend line, not a lab measurement — watch the direction over time, not the exact number.
If a Garmin VO₂max is shown too, note it's a running-specific watch estimate and tends to read low for non-running sports like MTB — AlForks' own estimate is the whole-body one.
Calorieskcal
An estimate of energy burned. With heart rate it's zone-weighted; otherwise it's based on the activity's duration, sport and climbing. It scales with your body weight, so keep your weight log current — a stale weight makes the calorie figure drift.
Aerobic fitnessZ2 HR trend
An indirect fitness indicator from your easy-effort (Zone 2) heart rate. As you get fitter, your HR at a given easy pace drifts down — so a line trending downward over months is progress. Read the trend, not week-to-week wiggles.
Training balancetraining mix · polarization
How your effort splits across easy, moderate and hard. Most endurance plans favour plenty of easy and some genuinely hard, with little stuck in the middle — this tells you whether your mix leans too hard, too easy, or sits about right.
Consistency
How regularly you've been active over the recent window — rewarding steady, frequent activity over occasional big one-offs.

Body & wellness

Body weightweight log
Your logged weight over time. Stored internally in kilograms; you can enter it in stones & pounds. Beyond the trend, it feeds the Calories estimate — which is why the card nudges you when the latest reading is over a week old.
Body BatteryGarmin
Garmin's 0–100 estimate of your energy reserves, blending stress, rest and activity. Higher means more in the tank. Shown when Garmin wellness data is connected.
Sleep
Last night's sleep duration from Garmin, in hours — a rough input to how recovered you are coming into the day.
Training statusGarmin
Garmin's own verdict on where your training is heading (productive, maintaining, detraining, and so on). Passed through as-is when available — AlForks' Fitness, Fatigue and Form are its independent take.

Trails & routes

On-trail matching
AlForks snaps your GPS track to named trails and roads from OpenStreetMap, so it can tell which trails you rode — not just where you went. This is what powers coverage, leaderboards and route detection.
Coverage% covered
How much of a trail's mapped length you actually rode on a given pass, as a percentage. Partial laps and out-and-backs read below 100%.
Completed
A trail counts as completed on a pass when you covered at least 85% of its length — or when you touched both ends with solid partial coverage (an “endpoint” completion), which catches trails where GPS drift leaves a gap in the middle.
Directionup / down / ↔
Which way you travelled a trail — uphill, downhill, or forward/reverse along its mapped line. Times are tracked per direction, since a descent and a climb of the same trail aren't really comparable.
Visits
How many separate times you've ridden a given trail (in a given direction) across all your logged activities.
Leaderboard & rankBest Run
Your completed passes of a trail or route, sorted fastest-first. Rank is where a given run sits in that list and Best Run is your fastest. Since it's only you, it's a personal leaderboard — a way to chase your own times.
DifficultyMTB scale
A trail's technical grade where OpenStreetMap provides one (the MTB difficulty scale). Blank when the trail isn't rated upstream.
Routes & attempts
A route is a named string of trails you ride as a set. An attempt is one activity that rode that route; Best is your fastest full attempt. Coverage decides whether an attempt counts as a full completion or a partial.
Suggested routes
Routes AlForks proposes by spotting loops and trail combinations you ride often, so your regulars become reusable named routes without building them by hand.

Data quality & processing

Duplicateslikely duplicate
When two activities cover nearly the same time and place — common if a ride syncs from both Strava and Garmin — AlForks flags them so you can keep one and dismiss the rest. Dismissed pairs stay remembered so they don't nag you again.
Spike repairraw vs repaired speed
GPS occasionally jumps, producing impossible speed spikes. Spike repair smooths those out so they don't wreck your max-speed and distance numbers. The activity view can show raw vs repaired speed side by side.
Trimtrim ends
Cuts unwanted start/end portions of a track — a recording left running in the car park, say — from the stats and map, without touching the original file.
Smoothingsmooth GPS
Optional extra smoothing of a noisy track over a chosen distance window, to tame jitter in the elevation and speed traces.
Data issuesodd times · missing type
Flags the app raises for review: odd times (gaps or out-of-order timestamps), missing type (no activity type set yet), and other quality warnings, gathered on the review screen.
Excluded from stats
A switch that keeps an activity in your library but leaves it out of totals, records and trends — handy for a test recording, a duplicate you want to keep, or a wildly broken track.
